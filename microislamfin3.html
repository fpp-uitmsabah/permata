1. Murabaha (Cost-Plus Financing) - Detailed Analysis

Contract Structure

Murabaha is the most widely used Islamic financing product in microfinance. The institution purchases an asset on behalf of the client and sells it at a markup, with payment made in installments.

Client Request

Client identifies an asset (equipment, inventory, etc.) and requests financing

Promise to Purchase

Client promises to buy from the institution at an agreed markup

Institution Purchases

Institution buys the asset from the supplier

Sale to Client

Institution sells asset to client at cost + agreed markup

Repayment

Client repays in agreed installments over specified period

Profit Calculation Formula:

Total Selling Price = Cost Price + Markup

Markup = Cost Price × Profit Margin × (Term in Months / 12)

Example:

Cost: RM 10,000 | Margin: 12% p.a. | Term: 12 months
Markup = 10,000 × 0.12 × (12/12) = RM 1,200
Selling Price = RM 11,200
Monthly Payment = 11,200 ÷ 12 = RM 933.33

Shariah Compliance Requirements:

  • • Institution must take actual ownership of the asset before selling
  • • Asset must exist and be identified at time of sale
  • • Selling price must be fixed and disclosed upfront
  • • Cannot charge penalty on late payment (only compensation for actual costs)

2. Mudharabah (Profit-Sharing Partnership)

Partnership Model

In Mudharabah, the financier (Rab al-Maal) provides capital while the entrepreneur (Mudarib) provides labor and expertise. Profits are shared according to a pre-agreed ratio.

Financier's Role (Rab al-Maal)

  • ✓ Provides 100% capital
  • ✓ Bears financial loss (if any)
  • ✓ Limited involvement in management
  • ✓ Shares profit as per agreement

Entrepreneur's Role (Mudarib)

  • ✓ Provides labor and expertise
  • ✓ Manages the business
  • ✓ Bears loss of time/effort
  • ✓ Shares profit as per agreement

Profit Distribution Example:

Scenario:

Capital: RM 50,000 | Agreed Ratio: 60:40 (Institution:Entrepreneur)
Business Profit: RM 15,000

Institution receives: 15,000 × 60% = RM 9,000
Entrepreneur receives: 15,000 × 40% = RM 6,000

If business makes a loss of RM 5,000:
Institution bears: RM 5,000 (full loss)
Entrepreneur gets: RM 0 (loses time and effort)

Best Suited For:

Trading Businesses

Buy and sell of goods

Service Ventures

Expertise-based income

Short-term Projects

Clear profit cycles

3. Musharakah (Joint Venture Financing)

Equity Partnership

Both the financier and entrepreneur contribute capital and may participate in management. Profits and losses are shared based on capital contribution or as mutually agreed.

Types of Musharakah:

Permanent Musharakah

Ongoing partnership with no predetermined exit date

Diminishing Musharakah

Financier's share gradually decreases as client buys out ownership

Diminishing Musharakah Example:

Asset Purchase (Equipment worth RM 100,000):

Institution contributes: RM 80,000 (80% ownership)
Client contributes: RM 20,000 (20% ownership)

Monthly Payments:
1. Rental payment for using institution's share
2. Purchase installment to buy institution's ownership

After 24 months of RM 3,500/month payments:
Client ownership increases to: 80%
Institution ownership decreases to: 20%

Process continues until client owns 100%

Key Features:

  • • Both parties contribute capital
  • • Profit sharing ratio can differ from capital ratio (by agreement)
  • • Loss sharing must be proportional to capital contribution
  • • Either or both parties may manage the business

4. Qard Hasan (Benevolent Loan)

Interest-Free Charitable Lending

A completely interest-free loan where the borrower only repays the principal amount. This is considered an act of charity in Islam.

When to Use Qard Hasan:

Emergency Situations:

  • • Medical emergencies
  • • Natural disaster relief
  • • Urgent family needs

Welfare Purposes:

  • • Education financing
  • • Start-up capital for very poor
  • • Debt consolidation

Comparison: Qard Hasan vs Commercial Loan

Aspect Qard Hasan Commercial Loan
Interest Zero Yes (10-20%)
Repayment Amount Principal only Principal + Interest
Purpose Charitable/Welfare Commercial
Flexibility High (grace periods allowed) Low (strict terms)

Note: While Qard Hasan generates no profit, institutions may charge administrative fees to cover operational costs. These fees must be reasonable and not profit-driven.

7. Real-World Application Scenarios

Scenario 1: Tailoring Equipment

Client: Fatimah, a seamstress who needs a RM 5,000 sewing machine
Recommended Product: Murabaha
Rationale: Specific asset needed, predictable income from tailoring, fixed repayment capability

Structure: Institution buys machine for RM 5,000, sells to Fatimah for RM 5,600 (RM 100/month for 12 months). She owns the machine immediately while repaying.

Scenario 2: Fish Trading Business

Client: Ahmad, experienced fish trader needing RM 20,000 for inventory
Recommended Product: Mudharabah
Rationale: Variable profits, requires expertise, short business cycles

Structure: Institution provides RM 20,000 capital, Ahmad manages trading. Profits split 60:40 (Institution:Ahmad). Monthly settlement based on actual sales.

Scenario 3: Medical Emergency

Client: Siti, factory worker needing RM 3,000 for child's surgery
Recommended Product: Qard Hasan
Rationale: Emergency welfare need, limited repayment capacity

Structure: Interest-free loan of RM 3,000, repayable at RM 250/month for 12 months. Flexible if financial difficulties arise.
